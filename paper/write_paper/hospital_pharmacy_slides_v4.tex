\section{Synthesis}

\begin{frame}{How the Results Connect}

\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.45em}
    \item \textbf{Hospital-level level-count estimates are negative, but log estimates are close to zero.}
    TWFE count and PPML estimates are negative for public hospitals after reform, while both $\ln(count+1)$ and $\ln(count)$ TWFE coefficients are small and statistically weak.

    \item \textbf{Hospital CSDID results do not show a strong average effect.}
    CSDID average ATT estimates are negative for count outcomes but imprecise; both log specifications are near zero across 1km, 2km, and 3km buffers.

    \item \textbf{County-level TWFE estimates are positive in levels.}
    Reform exposure is associated with higher county pharmacy counts and larger annual increases, with stable positive signs after adding controls and province-year fixed effects.

    \item \textbf{Scale matters for interpretation.}
    Hospital results compare public and non-public hospitals within local reform timing, while county results compare county-level pharmacy supply before and after reform adoption.

    \item \textbf{Dynamic diagnostics remain the main caution.}
    Count-outcome event studies are shown in both regular and $[-3,3]$ windows; log-outcome event studies are shown in the regular window only. Pre-period deviations should be reported as a limitation.

    \item \textbf{Overall pattern.}
    The reform coincides with broader county-level pharmacy expansion, but the hospital-level evidence does not support a clear relative increase in pharmacy entry around public hospitals.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}
